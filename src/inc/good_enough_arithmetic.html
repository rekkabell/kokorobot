Approximate answers are often good enough, even if technically wrong. Don Normal, The Design of Everyday Things

"Good Enough Arithmetics" permits us to estimate the answer to basic arithmetic equations in our heads with reasonable accuracy, without using a pencil and paper.

Temperature conversions

Say you are a Canadian visiting the United States and are unfamiliar with temperature readings in Fahrenheit, how do you convert it back to Celsius to know if you ought to wear a sweater?

The equation to make the conversion is rather complex to do in the head...

°C > °F   multiply by 9/5 and add 32.
°F > °C   subtract 32 and multiply by 5/9

Examples

What is 25 Celsius in Farenheit?

25 x 9 / 5 + 32 = 77 °F

What is 56 Fahrenheit in Celsius?

56 - 32 x 5 / 9 = 13.3 °C

As stated above, the above formula is very complex. For most purposes, anything within 5 °F of the real value works. To do a simple conversion without a calculator using the following simplified formula:

°F > °C   subtract 30 and divide by 2

An example:

What is 65°F in Celsius?

(65-30)/2= 17.5 °C

Multiplying Big Numbers

Most people cannot multiply big numbers in their heads. Like when estimating temperature, estimating the answer by rounding up(or down) number is good enough for most purposes.

Say you want to multiply 54 x 302, change 54 to 50 and 302 to 300.

50 x 300 = 15000
 5 x 3   = 15, then add back the tree zeros

The accurate answer is 16,308. The above estimate is 14% too large, but as previously stated, that is often good enough. In the above equation, I changed 54 to 50, removing 4. To get a more precise result, take 15000 and add 4x300 to the answer:

Simplify using smaller numbers: 4 x 3 = 12
Add back the two zeros to make a total of 1200
15000 + 1200 = 16,200

The above is now way more precise, without being 100% accurate. If you rounded the numbers up instead of down, substract instead.

When precision and accuracy are desired, use a calculator.
