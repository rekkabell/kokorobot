How does one go about adressing a non-binary person in French, a language riddled with gender?

  • Use neutral pronouns
    iel, ol, ul, ælle, al, ille, or ael. Or using the person's name
  • Use neutral articles
    an (un/une)
  • Modify word terminations
    Autaire for auteur/autrice
  • Portmanteaux
    frœur pour frère/sœur, toustes pour tous/toutes
  • Modification of conjugations
    Using punctuation such as acteur.ice, acteur·ice, acteur-ice, etc.

Conjugation

For word conjugations, combine masculine and feminine word endings by using a period, a middle dot, a hyphenation point, or a hyphen.

  • Period. Acteur.ice, heureux.se
  • Middle dot. Acteur·ice, heureux·se
  • Hyphenation point. Acteur‧se, heureux‧se
  • Hyphen. Acteur-ice, heureux-se

It is important to note that middle points or periods may cause problems for people who use screen readers. Hyphens, hyphenation points and apostrophes are more inclusive and don't cause breaks in the text.

In general, it is better to use epicene formulations such as les adultes, les élèves, le personel, la population, and les journalistes, rather than words like entraineur, directrice, etc.

Doubling words

When writing a letter or addressing a crowd, not defaulting to masculinity is good practice. Some documents include both the feminine and masculine words, such as saying "les Québécois et les Québécoises", but this formulation is not inclusive to non-binary people. It is better to ue neologisms and different phrasing.

Example:

À toustes les résidents du Québec...
À toustes les Québécoi·zeuh (adding an audible break and putting emphasis on the inclusive punctuation. Used mainly in oral speech, although it may not be perceived as an error.)

In speech, deforming words terminating with é·e aloud, for example saying:

Je suis fatiguaé(pronounced fati-gah-é).

But again, this may be perceived as bad pronounciation.

Abridged words

It is often possible to use an abridged version of the feminine and masculine terminations, foregoing mid-word punctuations. For example:

heureuxe or heureuxse, copaine, and cellui-ci(instead of celle-ci and celui-ci).

Mrs, Mme...

A preferred word is mondame, combining monsieur and madame. The english mix (Mx, for short) is also occasionally used in French.

Family terms

When refering to a mother or father, simply using the word parent is often enough.

Example: "Mon parent n'est pas à la maison."

I personally like to use the word mapa, a combination of mama and papa, which combines both and feels more casual, less austere.

  • cousin, cousine : cousaine, cousan
  • frère, sœur : frœur (one of my favorites)
  • neveu, nièce : nevèce, ness
  • parrain, marraine : parraine, marrain
  • tonton, tata : tonti, taton
